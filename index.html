Meerkats (Suricata suricatta)

This page talks about a particular species from the Mongoose family, if you would like to read up more on the Mongoose family please click here.

Native to the deserts of southern Africa, Meerkats, also known as Suricates, are small, diurnal mammals that belong to the Mongoose family(Herpestidae). They have a broad heads, large eyes, a pointed snout, and a long tapering tail. Their fur is typically brown with streaks of various shades, with a >dark stripe running down their back. They live in large social groups known as mobs or gangs and are known for their cooperative behaviour and for standing on their hind legs to survey their surroundings.

Meerkats are intelligent

Meerkats are highly social animals and have adapted to living in the harsh dry environments of southern Africa, often found near woody vegetation. They will be in mobs/gangs of 2-30, often of even male/female numbers, sometimes more if the environment permits, relying on cooperative behaviour for survival.

They have a hierarchy where the dominant Meerkats get to breed while the non-breeding Meerkats teach the pups how to behave. taking turns watching for predators while others look for food communicating through barks and chirps..working together to achieve a common goal and solve complex problems.

Adaptation

They're equipped with claws on their front paws that allow them to dig burrows and dig for food like worms or other insects, they are also very fast runners who have high mobility which make it much harder for predators to catch them. Meerkats are opportunistic feeders; they're not picky eaters. They will eat bugs rodents, carcasses if food supply has been short.

Burrowing for Meerkats is crucial.

A Meerkat's burrow can be up to 20 feet long and protect them from the harsh desert environment as the cooler underground temperatures help the Meerkats stay cool. The burrow openings are easily defended from predators as those walls are tighter than the rest. The burrows are often complex, with rooms for raising young, sleeping, or storing food. Newborn Meerkats will stay in these burrows for the first few weeks of their lives.

As most of a Meerkat's life is centred around burrowing and sunbathing, for them to survive their native conditions, they must be able to excavate.
